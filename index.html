This document summarises the team's engagement with the AIATSIS Core Cultural Learning (10 modules) and Culturally Responsive Teaching (5 units) programs, as shared on the Positive Partnerships Padlet review space.

Contributors: Jen Cousins, Lynda, Michael, Renee, Rachael, Jess, Deb, Emma D, Vanessa, Rani, and others from the PP team. Contributions span June 2025 to February 2026.

Question 1

What have people discussed in this Padlet?

Identity, belonging and the right to claim heritage

Stan Grant's video "What Part Are You?" sparked the most discussion across the whole Padlet. Contributors reflected on the experience of having one's Aboriginal identity questioned — particularly within Australia but not outside it — and the implicit racism embedded in asking someone to "prove" their heritage. Contributors also discussed how this intersects with financial assumptions and the experience of being perceived as "too white" or "too western."

Connection to Country — ways of knowing and being on land

Teams discussed freshwater and saltwater identities, kinship laws, seasonal frameworks, sign language, Kriol, and the complexity of what "connection to Country" really means — including its spiritual dimensions. Personal experiences visiting Country (Budj Bim, Brewarrina fish traps, remote community visits) enriched these discussions.

History, trauma and the Stolen Generations

The history modules prompted frank discussion about how recent and ongoing the impact of colonial policies is, the divergent narratives Australians were taught, and why these histories are hard to speak about. The Closing the Gap framework and its structural limitations were also discussed, including parallels with the NDIS.

Government policy — intent versus reality

The team worked through the timeline of Commonwealth Indigenous affairs policies, reflecting on how different governments have approached First Nations peoples through legislation — and how these shifts directly affect organisations like PP through funding and tendering cycles.

Native Title, Land Rights and legal complexity

This was identified as the most challenging content area. The team discussed the number of title types, the 10–30 year timeline for Native Title claims, the burden of proving "ongoing connection," and the compounding difficulty for people whose first language may not be English. The systemic catch-22 (less contact = better cultural outcome but harder legal navigation) was confronting.

Community engagement and the cultural interface

Module 8 generated strong discussion about what genuine community engagement looks like — two-way, circular, not FIFO consultation. The team reflected on PP's own FNCC role and the pre-workshop engagement Skye, Jay and the First Nations reference group undertake.

Language, deficit discourse and bias

Across multiple units the team discussed the power of language — Aboriginal English as a full language, codeswitching, the danger of deficit framing, and how unconscious bias operates. Strong connections were made to PP's neurodiversity-affirming language work throughout.

Culturally responsive teaching and the hidden curriculum

The Culturally Responsive Teaching units prompted discussion about how teachers embed their values in practice (the "hidden curriculum"), Indigenous pedagogies, UDL alignment, and what it means to make schools ready for students rather than the reverse.

Question 2

What have people found interesting?

Standout insight — shared by multiple contributors

Stan Grant's observation that his Aboriginal identity is never questioned outside Australia — he is simply accepted as Aboriginal elsewhere in the world. Multiple team members described this as the most memorable moment of the whole learning experience.

There have been 67 definitions of Aboriginality since European settlement

This fact surprised several contributors — highlighting how the colonial project has continually attempted to define and redefine identity on its own terms.

Freshwater and saltwater identities

New to many contributors — the significance of this distinction within First Nations communities, and how it shapes kinship and identity.

The Bundian Way and shared use of Country

Lynda's reflection on how First Nations community shared knowledge of safe coastal routes with early white settlers — an example of generosity and mutual living that challenges dominant narratives of conflict.

Budj Bim — the "simple, nomadic, hunters and gatherers" myth

Emma D's experience at the UNESCO-listed Gunditjmara eel farming systems made the falsity of this myth viscerally clear. Their guide's quiet invocation of it as they stood inside the evidence was particularly powerful.

Welcome to Country as a passport

Rani's reframing: Welcome to Country is a formal binding allowance by the Owners of Country — it can be limited, denied, and revoked. It also reaches back to ancestors, giving it a deeper intergenerational meaning many contributors had not considered.

Aboriginal English as a full language

The unit on language made clear that Aboriginal English has consistent rules and extensive vocabulary, and that speakers are multilingual. Reinforcing this — rather than treating it as "incorrect" English — is essential for dignity and cultural strength.

Spinifex grass and the world's strongest, thinnest condoms

Emma D shared this as a fun fact: the sharing of Indigenous knowledge about spinifex grass has led to a research partnership in Queensland producing a new manufacturing material — a tangible example of the contribution of First Nations knowledge to science and industry.

Intersecting bias and neurodivergent experience

Rani reflected on the resonances between the experience of being othered as a First Nations person and as a neurodivergent person — and how intersecting biases compound what individuals must push through.

The hidden curriculum

The concept that teachers silently signal what "good learning" looks like through their choices — and that this can perpetuate dominant-culture assumptions — was seen as directly relevant to PP's work and to the UDL Framework 3.0.

Question 3

What questions do people have?

Reflective question for the Framework (Renee)

"What do you think you need to know about land rights, native title or cultural heritage in your work?" — proposed as a question to include in the Framework document to prompt reflection among PP staff and partners.

Could a treaty have prevented the complexity of Native Title?

Emma D found herself wondering whether a treaty would have meant the legal landscape around Land Rights was less fragmented and complex. Raised not as a settled view but as a genuine reflection prompted by the module.

When is a question about heritage genuine curiosity vs. a challenge to identity?

Deb offered a thoughtful counterpoint to the Stan Grant discussion: intent and context matter. Not every question about heritage is an attempt to make someone prove themselves — sometimes it is genuine interest. The question raised: how do we hold space for both realities?

How do we navigate language when there is no 100% consensus?

Vanessa and Emma D noted that across both First Nations and disability/neurodiversity contexts, there is no single agreed language — and respecting individual choice is paramount. The question is: how do organisations like PP model this in practice?

Should PP reach out to specific community groups?

Emma D flagged several groups from the History of Indigenous Education unit as worth contacting — left open as a question for the working group to progress.

Should Narragunnawali be linked in PP's PL programs?

Emma D raised the question of whether Reconciliation Australia's Narragunnawali: Reconciliation in Education program should be included as a resource in PP's professional learning offerings.

Where do we place the onus when students are "failing"?

The flip question from Unit 3 reframed the issue: "Are Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students currently failing at school, or are our schools currently failing Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students?" This framing has direct application to PP's work.

Question 4

What ideas have been shared?

Make the parallels between First Nations and neurodivergent experience explicit in our Framework

Multiple contributors independently identified connections between their learning and PP's work — on deficit discourse, language, bias, being othered, and community engagement. This was not a passing observation; it emerged consistently across many modules and units. The idea: rather than treating these as separate domains, PP's Framework should name and draw on the underlying shared principles.

"The process is as important as the outcome" — as a PP value

Emma D highlighted this quote from Module 8 as relevant across all communities PP works with — not just First Nations — and playfully suggested it as a candidate for the next round of PP shirts. More seriously, it points to a value that could be made explicit in the Framework.

Use Aboriginal place names more consistently

Renee highlighted this as a simple, low-effort action with meaningful cultural impact — in PP's written materials, event names, and everyday language.

Yuendumu cost-benefit model as advocacy evidence

Rani described the Yuendumu analysis as a "win win" — it demonstrates deep personal and cultural benefit from community-controlled funding, expressed in terms that colonial funding systems can also understand. This is useful for PP's advocacy work.

Localised processes as a solution to legal complexity

Emma D proposed that localising processes (rather than applying national one-size-fits-all approaches) could help address the complexity of Native Title and cultural heritage — an idea with implications for how PP designs its First Nations engagement processes.

Include First Nations reference group voices more explicitly in resources

Emma D flagged a resource development consideration: historically, research about Indigenous peoples was dominated by white male researchers. PP has an opportunity to model a different approach — one that centres First Nations voices in how resources are created and reviewed.

Proactive preparation for guest speakers as a named PP practice

The module's guidance on supporting guest speakers (proactively checking for transport, access, refreshment and other needs rather than waiting to be asked) was identified as something PP already does well — and should be explicitly named in the Framework as a standard practice.

Sky in Acknowledgements of Country

Emma D's small but meaningful observation: most Acknowledgements of Country reference land, sea and waterways — but the sky is often omitted. Worth reflecting on in PP's own acknowledgement practice.

Question 5

Actions to apply to our work supporting First Nations autistic young people

Key principle from the learning

"What works for Indigenous students will support all students" — and we would add: the reverse is also true. What supports autistic young people in culturally safe ways draws on the same underlying principles as what supports First Nations young people. This intersection is where PP's unique value lies.

Language practice

Apply neurodiversity-affirming language principles alongside First Nations language principles — both resist deficit framing, both require respecting individual and community choice. Brief staff on how these overlap in practice.

Identity and belonging

Recognise that First Nations autistic young people may face compounding experiences of being "othered" — having both their identity and their neurology questioned or doubted. PP's resources and programs should validate identity without requiring proof.

Strengths-based framing

Consistently use strength-based language in all communications. Avoid deficit framing of both First Nations communities and autistic young people — and name this as a deliberate, connected practice.

Hidden curriculum

Review PP's programs and resources for hidden curriculum assumptions — what do our materials implicitly signal about what "good" participation, communication or family life looks like? Audit with a First Nations and neurodiversity lens.

Community engagement process

Pre-workshop community engagement (as undertaken by Skye, Jay and the First Nations reference group) should be understood by all PP staff as essential — not optional preparation. The process is as important as the workshop itself.

Cultural safety in programs

Draw on the two-way, circular engagement model from Module 8 to ensure PP's programs create genuine cultural safety — not just checkbox inclusion. Every step should be bidirectional.

Resources reflecting community voices

Prioritise First Nations autistic voices in resource development. Establish review processes that centre these voices, informed by the Unit 4 guide on selecting and evaluating resources.

Connection to Country in programs

Where possible, incorporate connection to Country content into PP's programs — recognising that for First Nations young people, cultural identity and Country are inseparable from wellbeing and belonging.

Question 6

Actions to apply to our own cultural learning

Commit to ongoing unlearning

Treat cultural learning as a continuous process of unlearning, not a one-time training. Create regular team touchpoints to share new insights — the Padlet model worked well and should continue.

Use Aboriginal place names

Begin incorporating Aboriginal place names into PP's everyday language — in emails, event references, and communications. Start with the places PP works most regularly.

Revisit and refresh Acknowledgements of Country

Reflect on PP's own Acknowledgement practice. Consider including the sky. Review whether our Acknowledgements are living statements of commitment or rote recitations.

Watch: In My Blood It Runs

Pre-2020 staff may not have seen this documentary (available on Binge and Apple TV), referenced in Unit 5. Consider as a team learning activity — especially Margaret Kemarre Turner's challenge to make schools ready for children.

Visit Country where possible

Multiple contributors noted that direct experiences on Country were among the most powerful learning moments (Budj Bim, remote community visits, walking on Country with FPDN). Build in opportunities for team members to access these experiences.

Reflect on our own bias regularly

Unconscious bias work is ongoing. The Unit 1 reflection that 3 in 4 Australians hold negative bias against First Nations people applies to us too. Build structured bias reflection into team practice.

Explore the Healing Foundation's Stolen Generations map

Use the corrected link: healingfoundation.org.au/stolen-generations/institutions-map — a powerful resource for understanding the geographic and institutional scope of the Stolen Generations.

Explore BOM's Indigenous weather knowledge

Emma D saved this link for the team: bom.gov.au/resources/indigenous-weather-knowledge — a useful resource connecting Country, seasons and Indigenous knowledge systems.

Question 7

Actions to apply to our First Nations Framework

Overall direction

The team's learning strongly suggests the Framework update should: (1) make explicit the connections between First Nations and neurodiversity principles, (2) embed process as a value — not just outcome, (3) centre self-determination and community control as organising principles, and (4) include practical tools and reflective questions for staff.

Add a Glossary of key terms

Emma D (Unit 1) flagged the Glossary from the Confronting Bias unit as a strong candidate for inclusion in the Framework document — ensuring shared language across the team and partners.

Include the cultural interface and engagement process model

The two-way, circular engagement graphic from Module 8 should be incorporated with references. This is particularly relevant for PP's pre-workshop engagement approach and for PP5 planning.

Add a reflective question on land rights and native title

Renee's proposed question: "What do you think you need to know about land rights, native title or cultural heritage in your work?" — for inclusion as a staff reflection prompt.

Incorporate content from Module 3 around January 26

Renee flagged, following Framework working group discussions in January 2026, that Module 3 (History Lives in Us) contains content relevant to communications around 26 January. This should be reviewed and incorporated appropriately.

Name "process is as important as outcome" as a PP value

This principle — drawn from Module 8 — applies across all PP's work with communities. The Framework should make it explicit.

Explicitly connect First Nations and neurodiversity-affirming principles

Deficit discourse, language, identity, bias and community engagement all surface as shared principles across both areas of PP's work. The Framework should name this connection — it is not just an analogy, it reflects shared underlying values.

Include the resource evaluation guide

The foundational considerations framework from Unit 4 (Selecting Resources) should inform PP's resource development and selection processes — particularly for the First Nations reference group.

Name proactive guest speaker support as a PP standard

PP already does this well. The Framework should document it explicitly: proactively checking for and arranging transport, access, refreshments and other needs — not waiting to be asked.

Reference YASA/SAFT and mental health links

Emma D flagged strong links in Module 10 to YASA/SAFT and mental health content that should be acknowledged in the Framework.

Consider Narragunnawali for PL programs

Reconciliation Australia's Narragunnawali: Reconciliation in Education program could be linked in PP's professional learning offerings.

Advocate for community-controlled funding models

The Yuendumu cost-benefit evidence from Module 5 supports PP's advocacy for directing funding to Aboriginal Community Controlled Organisations. The Framework should reflect this as a principled position.

Use Aboriginal place names in all PP communications

A simple, consistent practice. The Framework should name this as a standard expectation.
