Key Insights

8 Things That Surprised the Team

Fresh knowledge that challenged existing assumptions and expanded the team's understanding.

01

Welcome to Country is a formal passport — not a ceremony

Rani's reframing: Welcome to Country is a formal, binding allowance by the Owners of Country. It can be limited, denied, and revoked. It also reaches back to ancestors, giving it a deeper intergenerational meaning most contributors had not previously considered.

Identity
02

Freshwater and saltwater identities are distinct and significant

New to many contributors — the significance of this distinction within First Nations communities and how it shapes kinship, identity and relationship to Country in ways that go far deeper than geography.

Country
03

Budj Bim proves the "simple nomads" myth is false

Emma D's experience at the UNESCO-listed Gunditjmara eel farming systems made the falsity of the "simple, nomadic, hunters and gatherers" myth viscerally clear. Standing inside the evidence — a sophisticated, permanent aquaculture system — was particularly powerful.

History
04

Aboriginal English is a full language — not "incorrect" English

Aboriginal English has consistent rules and extensive vocabulary. Speakers are multilingual. Treating it as "incorrect" English rather than a language in its own right is harmful to dignity and cultural strength. This resonated strongly with PP's neurodiversity-affirming language work.

Language
05

The hidden curriculum signals what "good learning" looks like

Teachers silently communicate what counts as good participation through their choices — and this can perpetuate dominant-culture assumptions. Directly relevant to PP's work and to the UDL Framework 3.0. The question becomes: whose standards does the curriculum serve?

Education
06

Spinifex grass — from Indigenous knowledge to world's thinnest condoms

Emma D shared this as a striking example: sharing of Indigenous knowledge about spinifex grass has led to a research partnership in Queensland producing a new manufacturing material — a tangible, contemporary example of First Nations knowledge contributing to science and industry.

Indigenous Knowledge
07

The Bundian Way — generosity and shared Country

Lynda's reflection on how First Nations communities shared knowledge of safe coastal routes with early white settlers challenges dominant narratives of conflict and offers a different story of the possibility of mutual living on Country.

Country · History
08

Intersecting biases compound what individuals must push through

Rani reflected on the resonances between being othered as a First Nations person and as a neurodivergent person — two forms of identity questioning that compound each other. This intersection is precisely where PP's unique contribution lies.

Neurodiversity · Identity

Discussion Themes

What the Team Talked About Most

The recurring themes that emerged across all 15 modules and units, and the discussions they sparked.

Identity & Belonging

Who gets to define Aboriginality? The burden of "proving" heritage, 67 definitions, and what it means to belong.

Connection to Country

Freshwater & saltwater identities, kinship laws, seasonal frameworks, and the spiritual dimensions of Country.

History & Ongoing Trauma

Stolen Generations, divergent national narratives, and why these histories remain hard — and necessary — to speak about.

Government Policy

Intent vs. reality across successive Commonwealth approaches — and how funding shifts directly affect organisations like PP.

Language & Deficit Discourse

Aboriginal English as a full language; codeswitching; the danger of framing communities through deficit; unconscious bias.

Community Engagement

Two-way, circular engagement vs. FIFO consultation. What genuine partnership with communities actually looks like.

Native Title & Legal Complexity

10–30 year timelines, the burden of proof in a language that may be your 4th or 5th, and the systemic catch-22.

Culturally Responsive Teaching

Hidden curriculum, Indigenous pedagogies, UDL alignment — and making schools ready for students, not the reverse.

Shared Principles

Where First Nations and Neurodiversity Principles Converge

One of the most consistent insights across the team: the underlying values in First Nations cultural learning and neurodiversity-affirming practice are not just analogous — they are the same principles.

Resist deficit framing

Both First Nations communities and autistic people face narratives defined by what they "lack." Both frameworks insist on strength-based, asset-focused language.

Respect individual identity — don't require proof

Whether Aboriginality or neurodivergent identity: people should not be asked to prove who they are. Identity is self-determined.

Language is power — use it deliberately

Respecting individual and community choice in language; no single term fits all. PP must model this across both contexts.

Community control and self-determination

The Yuendumu model and PP's FNCC approach both demonstrate that community-directed processes deliver deeper, more lasting outcomes.

The process is as important as the outcome

Named in Module 8 and echoed across the team's reflections — how you engage matters as much as what you produce. A core PP value in the making.

What We're Doing About It

Actions Across Three Areas

The team identified specific, concrete actions for PP's work — across support for First Nations autistic young people, the team's own cultural learning, and the First Nations Framework.

Supporting First Nations autistic young people Our own cultural learning First Nations Framework
First Nations Autistic Young People

Validate identity without requiring proof

PP's resources and programs should recognise that First Nations autistic young people may face compounding experiences of being "othered" — both their identity and neurology questioned.

First Nations Autistic Young People

Apply neurodiversity-affirming & First Nations language together

Both resist deficit framing; both require respecting individual and community choice. Brief staff on how these principles overlap in practice.

First Nations Autistic Young People

Audit programs for hidden curriculum assumptions

What do PP's materials implicitly signal about "good" participation, communication or family life? Audit with a First Nations and neurodiversity lens.

Our Cultural Learning

Use Aboriginal place names in everyday language

Start with the places PP works most regularly. A simple, low-effort practice with meaningful cultural impact — in emails, event references, and everyday communication.

Our Cultural Learning

Revisit our Acknowledgement of Country

Consider including the sky — most Acknowledgements reference land, sea and waterways but omit it. Review whether our Acknowledgement is a living commitment or a rote recitation.

Our Cultural Learning

Keep the Padlet model going

Treat cultural learning as a continuous process of unlearning — not a one-time training. Create regular team touchpoints to share new insights. The Padlet model worked; continue it.

First Nations Framework

Make the shared principles explicit

The Framework should name the connection between First Nations and neurodiversity values — on deficit discourse, language, identity, bias and community engagement. It's not just an analogy.

First Nations Framework

Name "process is as important as outcome" as a PP value

This principle from Module 8 applies across all PP's work with communities. The Framework should make it explicit — and potentially build it into how PP evaluates its own programs.

First Nations Framework

Advocate for community-controlled funding models

The Yuendumu cost-benefit evidence supports directing funding to Aboriginal Community Controlled Organisations. The Framework should reflect this as a principled, evidenced position.
